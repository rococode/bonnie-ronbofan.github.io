Scaling Cloud Infrastructure: Lessons from Enterprise DaaS Deployments

Introduction

Enterprise cloud infrastructure in 2026 faces a paradox. Organizations demand the elasticity and cost efficiency of public cloud, yet their most critical workloads — virtual desktops serving tens of thousands of concurrent users — require the deterministic performance characteristics traditionally associated with on-premises data centers. Desktop-as-a-Service (DaaS) platforms sit squarely in the middle of this tension, and scaling them reliably is one of the hardest infrastructure problems in enterprise IT today.

At Citrix, I have spent years working on the infrastructure that powers virtual desktop delivery for some of the largest enterprises in the world. These deployments are not academic exercises. When a financial services firm needs 80,000 traders to have sub-10-millisecond session launch times at 7:30 AM Eastern on a Monday morning, or when a healthcare system needs to guarantee HIPAA-compliant desktop sessions across 200 hospitals during a pandemic surge, the margin for error is effectively zero.

This article distills the most important lessons I have learned about scaling cloud infrastructure for enterprise DaaS. These principles apply broadly to any stateful, latency-sensitive workload at scale, but the specifics are drawn from real-world experience with virtual desktop infrastructure serving millions of sessions.

Key Takeaways

  • Hybrid multi-cloud is not optional for enterprise DaaS at scale — it is the only architecture that satisfies compliance, cost, and resilience requirements simultaneously.
  • Predictive scaling beats reactive scaling by 4-6x in session launch latency during peak ramps, but requires at least 90 days of baseline data to be effective.
  • Observability must be DaaS-aware — generic cloud monitoring misses session-level metrics that are the earliest indicators of degradation.
  • Disaster recovery for stateful desktops requires fundamentally different strategies than stateless web applications.
  • Cost optimization and performance are not opposing forces when capacity planning is done correctly.

Understanding the Scale Challenge

Before diving into architecture, it is worth calibrating what "scale" means in the context of enterprise DaaS. A large deployment might serve 5 million or more virtual desktop sessions per month, with peak concurrency exceeding 500,000 simultaneous sessions across a globally distributed infrastructure. Each session is a stateful, interactive workload that consumes CPU, memory, GPU, storage IOPS, and network bandwidth in patterns that are fundamentally different from web application traffic.

Session Density and Resource Contention

The economics of DaaS depend on session density — how many virtual desktop sessions you can pack onto a single physical or virtual host. A typical knowledge worker session (Office applications, web browsing, email) consumes roughly 2 vCPUs, 4 GB RAM, and 15-25 storage IOPS at steady state. A power user session (CAD, financial modeling, video editing) can consume 8+ vCPUs, 32 GB RAM, a dedicated GPU slice, and 200+ IOPS. The blended density on a well-tuned host running 64 vCPUs and 256 GB RAM typically lands between 20 and 35 knowledge worker sessions or 4 to 8 power user sessions.

The challenge is that these are averages. Real workloads exhibit burst patterns that can spike resource consumption 3-5x above steady state. A user opening a large Excel spreadsheet with complex formulas can momentarily consume 8 vCPUs and 16 GB of RAM. If ten users on the same host do this simultaneously — which happens more often than you would expect at 9:15 AM when people open their morning reports — the host becomes CPU-constrained, and every session on that host experiences degradation.

At scale, the law of large numbers provides some natural smoothing, but it is not sufficient. We have observed that resource contention events significant enough to degrade user experience occur on approximately 3-4% of hosts during any given peak hour. At 500,000 concurrent sessions distributed across roughly 18,000 hosts, that means 540 to 720 hosts are experiencing contention at any moment during peak. Each affected host impacts 20-35 users, yielding 10,000 to 25,000 users experiencing degradation simultaneously. That is unacceptable.

Network Latency Considerations

Virtual desktop protocols are exquisitely sensitive to network latency and jitter. The Citrix HDX protocol, for example, delivers a responsive user experience at up to about 150ms round-trip latency for most workloads, but the experience degrades noticeably above 80ms for tasks involving rapid visual updates (scrolling, video playback, drawing). Jitter above 20ms causes visible artifacts regardless of baseline latency.

When your users are distributed globally but your compute infrastructure is concentrated in a handful of cloud regions, the physics of light speed becomes your constraint. A user in Singapore connecting to a desktop hosted in US-East will experience roughly 230ms of round-trip latency on a good day — well above the threshold for acceptable interactive performance. This fundamental constraint drives multi-region and edge deployment strategies that add significant architectural complexity.

Multi-Cloud Architecture Decisions

Every enterprise DaaS deployment of meaningful scale that I have worked on has ended up with a multi-cloud architecture, though the path to that conclusion varies. Some organizations start multi-cloud intentionally; others arrive there through acquisition, regulatory mandate, or hard-won operational experience with single-provider outages.

Why Hybrid Multi-Cloud Is Essential

Three forces make hybrid multi-cloud (typically AWS + Azure + on-premises) essential for enterprise DaaS at scale:

Regulatory compliance and data sovereignty. Financial services firms subject to OCC, PRA, or MAS regulations often cannot place certain workloads in public cloud at all, or can only use specific regions within specific providers. Healthcare organizations must ensure PHI remains within jurisdictions that satisfy their HIPAA and local privacy requirements. A European enterprise serving users across the EU, UK, and Switzerland may need compute in Frankfurt, London, and Zurich — no single cloud provider offers all three with consistent DaaS-optimized instance types.

Cost optimization through arbitrage. Cloud compute pricing for DaaS workloads varies significantly between providers, regions, and purchasing models. AWS Reserved Instances for M6i instances in us-east-1 price differently than Azure Reserved VM Instances for Dasv5-series in East US. More importantly, the spot/preemptible instance markets in each cloud offer different discount levels at different times. A multi-cloud architecture lets you place baseline capacity on reserved instances in the provider offering the best price for each region, while bursting onto whichever cloud has the cheapest available spot capacity. We have measured cost savings of 18-25% compared to single-cloud deployments when this arbitrage is executed well.

Resilience. Cloud providers experience region-level outages. AWS us-east-1 had five significant incidents in the past three years. Azure East US has had four. If your entire DaaS fleet is in a single provider, a region-level outage takes down tens of thousands of users with no mitigation path. Multi-cloud with cross-provider failover capability reduces the blast radius of any single provider incident.

Infrastructure as Code: The Foundation

Managing infrastructure across multiple clouds and on-premises environments without rigorous Infrastructure as Code (IaC) is operationally impossible at scale. We use Terraform as the primary IaC tool, with provider-specific modules for AWS (EC2, VPC, ELB, Route 53), Azure (Virtual Machines, VNet, Application Gateway, Azure DNS), and on-premises (vSphere provider for VMware environments).

The critical design decision is how to structure Terraform state. We use a workspace-per-environment model with remote state in Terraform Cloud, but with a custom abstraction layer that lets us express a "deployment" as a single logical unit that spans multiple cloud providers. A deployment manifest might specify: "I need 500 knowledge-worker hosts in AWS us-east-1, 300 in Azure West Europe, and 200 on-premises in the Ashburn data center, all running Windows Server 2025 with the Q1 2026 gold image." The abstraction layer translates this into provider-specific Terraform configurations, handles cross-provider networking (VPN tunnels, Transit Gateway, ExpressRoute), and ensures DNS and load balancing are configured consistently.

Trade-offs Worth Acknowledging

Multi-cloud is not free. It demands that your engineering team maintain expertise across multiple platforms. Your monitoring and observability stack must normalize metrics from different sources. Your networking team must manage cross-cloud connectivity that adds latency and cost. Your security team must enforce consistent policies across providers with different IAM models. We estimate that multi-cloud adds approximately 30-40% to the operational complexity of the platform compared to a single-cloud deployment. The benefits justify this cost at scale, but organizations running fewer than 10,000 concurrent sessions should carefully evaluate whether single-cloud with multi-region is sufficient.

Capacity Planning and Auto-Scaling

Capacity planning for DaaS is fundamentally different from capacity planning for stateless web applications. A web application can shed load gracefully — slower response times, queued requests, degraded features. A virtual desktop session either works or it does not. A user who clicks "launch desktop" and waits 45 seconds before getting an error message has experienced a service failure, not a degradation. This binary quality-of-experience model makes capacity planning both more critical and more difficult.

Predictive vs. Reactive Scaling

Reactive auto-scaling — the kind you get out of the box with AWS Auto Scaling Groups or Azure VMSS — responds to current resource utilization by adding or removing instances. For DaaS, this is necessary but profoundly insufficient. The problem is boot time. A bare-metal or cloud instance running a DaaS host image takes 4 to 8 minutes to boot, join the domain, register with the session broker, and begin accepting sessions. If your reactive scaler detects that you are at 85% session capacity and triggers a scale-up, those new hosts will not be available for 4-8 minutes. During morning login storms, you can burn through your remaining 15% capacity buffer in under 2 minutes.

Predictive scaling addresses this by forecasting capacity needs before demand materializes. Our implementation uses a time-series forecasting model trained on 90+ days of historical session data, decomposed into daily, weekly, and seasonal components. The model predicts session demand in 15-minute intervals for the next 24 hours, with confidence intervals. We provision to the 95th percentile of the predicted demand, plus a configurable buffer (typically 10-15%). This means hosts are booted, registered, and ready before users arrive.

The results speak for themselves. With purely reactive scaling, our P95 session launch time during the morning peak (8:00-9:30 AM local time) was 12-18 seconds, with approximately 2% of launches exceeding 30 seconds or failing entirely. With predictive scaling, P95 session launch time during the same period dropped to 3-4 seconds, with failure rates below 0.1%. The 4-6x improvement in launch latency translates directly to user satisfaction and help desk call volume.

Buffer Capacity Strategies

Even with predictive scaling, you need buffer capacity to handle unpredicted demand spikes — an all-hands meeting that drives everyone to their desktops simultaneously, a breaking news event that spikes activity in a trading floor, or simply a Monday after a long weekend when the model has less training data. We maintain three tiers of buffer:

  • Hot buffer (immediate): 10-15% of predicted peak capacity, consisting of fully booted and registered hosts with no active sessions. These can accept sessions within seconds. Cost: high, since you are paying for idle compute.
  • Warm buffer (2-4 minutes): Pre-provisioned cloud instances that are stopped but have their disks attached and domain-join cached. Starting these instances skips the image download and most of the boot process. We maintain 5-10% of peak capacity in warm buffer.
  • Cold buffer (8-15 minutes): Auto-scaling group capacity that launches fresh instances from the gold image. This is your "insurance policy" for truly unexpected demand. The auto-scaling group has pre-configured launch templates and pre-warmed AMIs/managed images in each region.

Cost Optimization

The naive approach to capacity planning — over-provision everything and keep it running — is economically ruinous at scale. At 500,000 peak concurrent sessions, even 5% over-provisioning represents approximately 900 unnecessary hosts. At an average cost of $0.35/hour per host (blended across instance types and purchasing models), that is $315/hour or $2.3 million per year in wasted compute.

Our approach optimizes cost along several dimensions. Reserved instances cover the baseline load — the minimum session count that we observe even during the lowest-demand periods (typically 30-40% of peak). Savings Plans cover the predictable daily ramp above baseline. On-demand and spot instances cover the variable peak above that. The ratio shifts seasonally — during summer months when many enterprises see lower utilization, we reduce reserved capacity and rely more on spot. The overall blended cost per session-hour has decreased 34% over the past two years through these optimizations.

Performance Optimization at Scale

Performance optimization for DaaS at enterprise scale involves a different set of levers than most cloud workloads. The critical path is the end-to-end chain from the user's endpoint device through the network, through the session broker, to the virtual desktop host, and back. Every component in this chain has optimization opportunities and failure modes.

Session Broker Optimization

The session broker is the control plane that matches users to available desktop sessions. At 500,000 concurrent sessions with churn rates of 15-20% per hour, the broker must process 75,000-100,000 session launches and teardowns per hour — roughly 25 per second sustained, with peaks of 200+ per second during morning login storms. The broker must query user entitlements, evaluate load-balancing policies, select an optimal host, and initiate the session, all within a latency budget of 500ms.

We have optimized broker performance through several techniques. Entitlement data is cached aggressively with a 60-second TTL and invalidated on policy changes. Host selection uses a pre-computed scoring algorithm that evaluates host health, current load, geographic proximity to the user, and affinity (whether the user had a previous session on that host with cached profile data). The scoring is updated every 10 seconds and stored in a Redis cluster, so the broker can select a host with a single cache lookup rather than querying each host in real time.

Image Management

Gold image management is often underestimated as a performance lever. At scale, the gold image — the master OS image from which all virtual desktops are provisioned — must be optimized for boot speed, session density, and application launch time. We maintain a rigorous image engineering pipeline that includes OS debloating (removing unnecessary services, scheduled tasks, and telemetry), application pre-installation with NGEN pre-compilation for .NET applications, Citrix Optimizer profiles, and disk defragmentation optimized for SSD/NVMe storage.

A well-optimized Windows Server 2025 gold image boots to login screen in 18-22 seconds on a cloud instance with NVMe storage, compared to 45-60 seconds for an unoptimized image. Over 100,000 daily session launches, that 25-35 second difference is meaningful — both for user experience and for the speed at which warm buffer capacity becomes available.

Protocol Tuning

The Citrix HDX protocol offers extensive tuning parameters that significantly impact user experience. The most impactful settings at scale include adaptive transport (which switches between TCP and EDT/UDP based on network conditions), lossy compression thresholds for image quality, and bandwidth allocation priorities across virtual channels (display, audio, USB redirection, clipboard, printing).

For wide-area deployments where users connect over the internet, we configure adaptive transport with EDT as the preferred protocol. EDT uses UDP, which avoids TCP head-of-line blocking and recovers from packet loss more gracefully. In our measurements, EDT reduces perceived latency by 25-40% compared to TCP for users on networks with 1-3% packet loss — which describes most home internet connections and many corporate WANs.

GPU Acceleration

GPU-accelerated workloads (CAD, 3D modeling, video production, data visualization) require dedicated GPU resources. We use NVIDIA vGPU technology with A10G and L4 GPUs on cloud instances, partitioned to provide between 1 GB and 16 GB of GPU framebuffer per session depending on the workload profile. The key performance consideration is GPU scheduling — NVIDIA's time-slicing scheduler provides fair sharing but adds latency jitter. For latency-sensitive workloads, we use MIG (Multi-Instance GPU) partitioning on A100 GPUs, which provides hardware-level isolation and eliminates scheduling jitter at the cost of coarser partitioning granularity.

Latency Budgets and SLI/SLO Definitions

We define Service Level Indicators (SLIs) and Service Level Objectives (SLOs) at each layer of the stack to create accountability and detect degradation early:

  • Session launch SLI: Time from user click to interactive desktop. SLO: P50 < 5s, P95 < 10s, P99 < 20s.
  • Input latency SLI: Time from keystroke/mouse click to visual feedback. SLO: P50 < 30ms, P95 < 80ms.
  • Frame rate SLI: Rendered frames per second during active screen updates. SLO: P50 > 30 fps, P5 > 15 fps.
  • Session reliability SLI: Percentage of sessions that complete without unexpected disconnection. SLO: > 99.5% per 24-hour window.

These SLOs are measured continuously and surfaced in real-time dashboards. Any SLO breach triggers an automated incident workflow.

Observability and Monitoring

Building observability into cloud infrastructure from day one is not optional — it is the single most important investment you can make in operational reliability. I have seen organizations attempt to bolt on monitoring after a production incident and the result is always incomplete coverage, inconsistent instrumentation, and alert fatigue.

The Observability Stack

Our observability stack is built on three pillars:

Metrics: We use Prometheus for metrics collection with a federated architecture. Each cloud region runs a Prometheus server that scrapes targets locally, and a central Thanos deployment aggregates metrics across regions for global views and long-term storage. Retention is 15 days at full resolution in Prometheus and 13 months downsampled in Thanos object storage. Grafana provides dashboards and alerting.

Logs: Application and infrastructure logs flow through Fluent Bit agents on each host into a centralized Elasticsearch cluster (we are evaluating migration to OpenSearch). Structured logging in JSON format is enforced across all services, with mandatory fields for correlation ID, session ID, user ID, and region. Log retention is 30 days hot, 90 days warm, and 365 days in cold storage for compliance.

Traces: Distributed tracing with OpenTelemetry spans the entire session launch path, from the user's click through the Citrix Gateway, session broker, hypervisor, and into the virtual desktop. Traces are collected by Jaeger and correlated with metrics and logs via the session ID. This end-to-end tracing has been invaluable for diagnosing latency regressions — we can pinpoint which component in the chain introduced delay and measure its contribution precisely.

DaaS-Specific Metrics

Generic cloud monitoring (CPU utilization, memory usage, network throughput) is necessary but misses the metrics that actually predict user experience degradation. We instrument the following DaaS-specific metrics that have proven to be the most actionable:

  • ICA RTT (round-trip time): The latency of the Citrix ICA protocol as measured within the session. This is the single best proxy for user-perceived responsiveness.
  • Session launch funnel: Conversion rates through each stage (authentication, enumeration, brokering, host selection, session creation, protocol handshake). Drop-offs at any stage indicate specific component issues.
  • Profile load time: Time to load the user's profile (Citrix Profile Management or FSLogix). Profile bloat is a leading cause of slow session launches and is invisible to infrastructure-level monitoring.
  • App launch time: Time from application shortcut click to interactive window within the session. Regression in app launch time often indicates image or antivirus issues.
  • IOPS per session: Storage I/O consumed per session, which predicts storage contention before it manifests as user-visible lag.

Managing Alert Fatigue

At 18,000+ hosts across multiple clouds and regions, even a 0.1% alert rate per host per hour produces 18 alerts per hour — enough to desensitize an on-call engineer within a single shift. We manage alert fatigue through aggressive alert aggregation (grouping related alerts by region, host pool, and failure mode), tiered severity levels (only P1 and P2 page on-call; P3 and below go to a queue), and error budget-based alerting where we alert on SLO burn rate rather than instantaneous threshold violations. If our session launch P95 SLO is 10 seconds and we are consuming our monthly error budget at 2x the sustainable rate, that triggers a warning. At 10x, it pages on-call. This approach dramatically reduces noise while catching real problems earlier.

Disaster Recovery and Business Continuity

Disaster recovery for DaaS infrastructure is fundamentally harder than DR for stateless applications because virtual desktop sessions are stateful. A user with 15 open applications, unsaved documents, and a specific desktop layout cannot simply be "failed over" to another region the way a stateless HTTP request can be routed to a different server. This reality shapes every aspect of our DR strategy.

RPO/RTO Targets

We define two classes of DR scenarios with different targets:

Control plane failure (session broker, database, authentication services): RPO = 0 (no data loss due to synchronous replication), RTO = 5 minutes (automated failover to standby control plane in a different availability zone or region). The control plane is stateless enough that hot standby with database replication achieves these targets reliably.

Data plane failure (host infrastructure in a region): RPO = session state is lost (users must relaunch sessions), RTO = 15 minutes (time for users to be redirected to alternate region and launch new sessions on pre-provisioned capacity). Persistent user data (documents, profile) has RPO = 15 minutes through asynchronous replication of FSLogix profile containers and OneDrive/SharePoint sync.

Multi-Region Failover

Our failover architecture maintains warm standby capacity in at least two geographically separated regions for each primary region. DNS-based traffic management (AWS Route 53 health checks + Azure Traffic Manager) detects control plane failures and redirects users within 60 seconds. The warm standby regions maintain 20% of the primary region's peak capacity in pre-booted hosts, with auto-scaling configured to rapidly expand to full capacity. In practice, a region-level failover results in a 2-3 minute disruption for users who had active sessions (they must reconnect and relaunch) and near-zero disruption for new session launches.

Chaos Engineering

We adopted chaos engineering principles from Netflix's pioneering work and adapted them for VDI infrastructure. Our chaos experiments include: randomly terminating session broker instances to verify control plane resilience, injecting network latency and packet loss between components to test protocol resilience, simulating storage failures on host clusters to verify session migration, and conducting full region evacuation drills quarterly.

The most valuable chaos experiment we run is what we call "Tuesday at 10" — every Tuesday at 10 AM UTC, we randomly terminate 1% of active sessions in a designated test host pool to verify that session reconnection, profile recovery, and user notification all work correctly. This continuous validation has caught three regressions in the past year that would have caused significant user impact during a real incident.

Lessons Learned

After years of scaling enterprise DaaS infrastructure, these are the lessons that have proven most broadly applicable:

1. Invest in Infrastructure Abstractions Early

The single highest-ROI investment we made was building a deployment abstraction layer that hides cloud-provider differences behind a consistent API. Engineers describe what they need (host pools with specific capacity, networking, and storage characteristics) without specifying how each cloud provider implements it. This abstraction paid for itself within six months by reducing the time to deploy a new region from three weeks to two days and eliminating an entire class of "forgot to configure the Azure equivalent of this AWS setting" errors.

Takeaway: Build your multi-cloud abstraction before you need it. Retrofitting is 5-10x more expensive than building it in from the start.

2. Treat Session Launch as a Product Metric, Not an Infrastructure Metric

For years, our primary infrastructure metrics were CPU utilization, memory pressure, and host availability. These are important but they do not capture what users actually care about — how quickly and reliably their desktop appears when they click "launch." When we made session launch time and success rate the primary metrics that the entire organization optimized for, we discovered and fixed issues that had been invisible in infrastructure metrics: slow DNS resolution, inefficient group policy processing, profile container fragmentation, and certificate validation delays.

Takeaway: Define your SLIs from the user's perspective and make them the top-level dashboard that everyone from engineers to executives monitors.

3. Capacity Planning Is a Continuous Activity, Not a Quarterly Exercise

Early on, we did capacity planning quarterly — reviewing growth trends, adjusting reserved instance purchases, and updating auto-scaling configurations. This cadence was too slow. A new customer onboarding 15,000 users, a VPN-to-DaaS migration at an existing customer, or a seasonal business cycle could change capacity requirements dramatically between planning cycles. We moved to continuous capacity planning with automated weekly model retraining and daily capacity adjustment recommendations.

Takeaway: Automate capacity planning and make it continuous. The model should retrain on fresh data at least weekly, and capacity adjustments should be reviewable daily.

4. Do Not Underestimate Profile and Data Management

In my experience, more DaaS performance issues originate from user profile and data management than from compute or network problems. A user whose FSLogix profile container has grown to 15 GB due to accumulated Outlook cache and Teams data will experience 30-45 second login delays regardless of how fast your compute infrastructure is. Profile management requires active governance — size limits, cache policies, container compaction schedules, and monitoring of profile load times as a first-class SLI.

Takeaway: Instrument profile load times, set size limits with enforcement, and run container compaction on a schedule. Profile management is infrastructure, not an afterthought.

5. Chaos Engineering Is Non-Negotiable at Scale

Every organization resists chaos engineering initially. The idea of deliberately breaking production systems feels irresponsible. But the alternative — discovering your failover does not work during a real incident — is far more irresponsible. Start small (terminate a single non-critical instance), build confidence, and expand scope gradually. The insights from controlled failure are qualitatively different from those you get from testing in staging environments.

Takeaway: Start chaos engineering in production with narrow blast radius experiments. Expand scope as your team builds confidence and your systems demonstrate resilience.

6. Cross-Functional Ownership Beats Siloed Operations

The most effective operational model we have found is cross-functional teams that own a complete vertical slice of the platform — from Terraform modules through host configuration through session broker logic through monitoring dashboards. When a single team owns the full stack for a particular capability (e.g., "GPU workloads" or "APAC region"), they develop deep understanding of how decisions at each layer affect the others. Contrast this with the traditional model where a "compute team" manages hosts, a "network team" manages connectivity, and a "monitoring team" manages dashboards — with no one owning the end-to-end user experience.

Takeaway: Organize teams around outcomes (user-facing capabilities or regions), not layers (compute, network, storage). Give each team full-stack ownership and accountability.

Conclusion

Scaling cloud infrastructure for enterprise DaaS is a discipline that sits at the intersection of distributed systems engineering, capacity planning, performance optimization, and operational excellence. There are no silver bullets — no single tool, framework, or cloud provider that solves the problem. Success comes from rigorous engineering practices applied consistently across every layer of the stack.

The principles outlined in this article — hybrid multi-cloud architecture, predictive capacity planning, user-centric SLIs, comprehensive observability, tested disaster recovery, and continuous chaos engineering — have been validated across deployments serving millions of users. They are not theoretical. They are the product of real incidents, real post-mortems, and real operational learning.

Looking ahead, I see three trends that will reshape enterprise DaaS infrastructure in the next two to three years. First, the emergence of purpose-built DaaS silicon (custom ASICs optimized for remoting protocol encoding) will change the economics of GPU-accelerated workloads. Second, edge computing will bring DaaS infrastructure closer to users, reducing latency for the "last mile" that is currently the biggest constraint on user experience. Third, AI-driven operations — moving beyond predictive scaling to fully autonomous infrastructure management — will reduce the operational burden of managing globally distributed DaaS platforms.

The organizations that invest in solid infrastructure foundations today will be best positioned to adopt these advances. The ones that cut corners will find themselves rebuilding from scratch. In enterprise infrastructure, there are no shortcuts — only deferred costs.
